Responsible Penny Hunting: Don't Ruin It for Everyone

On This Page

Penny hunting thrives on community, strategy, and discretion.

Acting irresponsibly not only gets you shut downâ€”it can cause stores to crack down harder on everyone.

Here's how to do it right.

1. Be Respectful to Store Employees

Even if you're frustrated, caught off-guard, or denied a penny sale:

  • Stay calm
  • Stay polite
  • Avoid confrontations or lectures

Staff are following ordersâ€”not making personal decisions against you.

They didn't create the penny item system. They didn't decide to pull your item. They're just doing their job.

Yelling at an employee or making a scene only:

  • Gets you remembered (in a bad way)
  • Increases the chances you'll be banned
  • Makes it harder for other penny hunters

Treat staff with respect, and they're more likely to look the other way in the future.

2. Don't Be Loud About Penny Finds

Getting loud, excited, or bragging at checkout draws attention.

So does:

  • Showing your receipt to other customers
  • Telling staff "You just scored a penny item"
  • Filming inside the store

The more attention you bring, the more likely stores are to tighten enforcement or train employees to spot penny hunters.

Keep your wins to yourself. Celebrate outside the store.

3. Use Community Resources, Don't Abuse Them

If you're part of a penny shopping Facebook group, forum, or Discord:

Do:

  • Share helpful info, like UPCs, tag dates, or clearance cycles
  • Post accurate findsâ€”not rumors
  • Help newbies who have genuine questions

Don't:

  • Flood the group with repeat questions or off-topic posts
  • Spread unconfirmed rumors â€” they waste everyone's time
  • Post fake finds for attention or engagement

Responsible sharing keeps these communities thriving and valuable.

4. Understand the Time Investment

This is not a get-rich-quick game.

Penny hunting requires:

  • Time
  • Patience
  • A lot of empty trips

You might check 3 stores and find nothing. You might spend an hour hunting and walk out empty-handed.

Don't expect huge savings every timeâ€”it's about the long game.

If you're not willing to put in the effort and accept the dry spells, this isn't for you.

5. Be Realistic About Value

Penny items are often clearance leftovers or oddball stock.

  • Some are useful
  • Others are niche or hard to resell
  • Many are discontinued for a reason

Don't "leave items for others" as a "nice gesture." The system will flag the item to be pulled and destroyed anyway.

If you find it and want it, buy it. If you don't want it, leave itâ€”but don't expect it to sit there for someone else. Staff will likely pull it soon.

6. Know When to Walk Away

If an employee or manager confronts you and denies the sale, just move on.

  • It's not worth getting banned from the store
  • It's better to lose one item than burn access to future deals

Pushing back aggressively might work once, but it'll get you flagged. And once you're flagged, every future visit becomes harder.

Choose your battles wisely.

Why This Matters

Every time someone causes a scene, films themselves at checkout, or harasses staff about penny items, stores respond by:

  • Training employees to watch for penny hunters
  • Implementing stricter removal policies
  • Pulling items faster before they hit the floor
  • Banning repeat offenders

Your actions don't just affect youâ€”they affect the entire community.

Final Thought

Penny hunting is part skill, part luck, and part community.

The people who last the longest are the ones who:

  • Stay low-key
  • Treat staff with respect
  • Share useful info without bragging
  • Know when to walk away

Play the long game. Don't burn it for a $0.01 item.

Community Guidelines for Penny Hunters

Beyond individual responsibility, we need community standards to keep penny hunting viable for everyone.

Share Knowledge, Not Heat

When contributing to online communities:

  • Post verified finds with SKUs, dates, and locations (city/state only)
  • Avoid posting store addresses or staff names/faces in photos
  • Don't share tactics that burn stores (e.g., exploiting specific employees)
  • Correct misinformation politely when you see it
  • Help beginners learn the ropes without gatekeeping

Respect Store Variations

Not all stores operate the same way:

  • Urban vs. rural stores have different inventory turnover
  • High-traffic locations pull clearance faster
  • Regional differences in cadence and enforcement exist
  • Manager personalities vary widelyâ€”adapt your approach

What works at one store may not work at another. Share observations, but don't assume universal tactics.

Support Fellow Hunters

  • Celebrate others' wins without jealousy
  • Answer beginner questions even if they've been asked before
  • Share unsuccessful hunts too so people know what to expect
  • Warn others if a store starts cracking down

Don't Ruin It for Others

These behaviors hurt the entire community:

  • Clearing shelves of every penny item (leave some for others)
  • Bragging on social media with store-identifying photos
  • Arguing with staff or causing scenes
  • Teaching unethical tactics (e.g., hiding items to buy later)
  • Recruiting too aggressively (flooding stores with hunters)

Code of Conduct for the Community

If you're part of penny hunting communities, follow these principles:

Honesty

  • Share accurate information based on your experience
  • Don't exaggerate finds or success rates
  • Admit when you're wrong or unsure
  • Correct your posts if information changes

Respect

  • Treat all community members with courtesy
  • Respect differing opinions on ethics and tactics
  • Don't belittle beginners or those with fewer finds
  • Avoid toxic behavior, personal attacks, or harassment

Responsibility

  • Think long-term about the impact of your actions
  • Prioritize community sustainability over personal gain
  • Call out harmful behavior when you see it
  • Lead by example in stores and online

Inclusivity

  • Welcome newcomers and answer their questions
  • Share knowledge freely without gatekeeping
  • Recognize that everyone has different goals (personal use vs. reselling)
  • Support diverse perspectives and approaches

How to Contribute Responsibly

Want to give back to the penny hunting community? Here's how:

Share Success Stories

Post your finds with:

  • SKU and UPC numbers
  • Date found and markdown date from tag
  • City and state (not specific store address)
  • Category and item description
  • What you learned from the experience

Document Patterns

Help others by tracking:

  • Markdown day patterns at your local stores
  • Which cadence (A or B) your region uses
  • Categories that frequently go penny
  • Seasonal trends and timing

Create Resources

Contribute to the community with:

  • Regional store lists with hunting notes
  • Custom tracking spreadsheets
  • Screenshot tutorials for using the app
  • Printable cheat sheets with local info

See the Contribute page for templates and detailed guidelines.

When to Walk Away (Expanded)

Beyond just denials at checkout, know when it's time to stop or take a break:

If You're Getting Too Aggressive

  • Visiting the same store daily
  • Arguing with staff regularly
  • Feeling entitled to penny items
  • Prioritizing penny hunting over other responsibilities

If a Store Cracks Down

  • You've been warned by management
  • Staff recognize you and watch closely
  • Multiple checkout denials in a row
  • Increased security presence when you're there

If It's No Longer Fun or Profitable

  • More stress than savings
  • Gas and time costs exceed finds
  • Dry spells lasting months
  • Negative impact on your mental health

Remember: This is supposed to be a hunt, not a battle. If it stops being worthwhile, it's okay to step back.
